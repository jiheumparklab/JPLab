About the Lab

The Jiheum Park Lab develops computational methods and clinically grounded systems that support precision medicine by enabling earlier disease detection, personalized screening, and data-informed treatment decisions. Our work focuses on learning from large-scale longitudinal health data—including electronic health records and genomic information—to model patient trajectories and identify meaningful patterns that can guide clinical care.

Drawing on multidisciplinary training in engineering, data science, and medicine, the lab bridges advanced computational approaches with the needs of real clinical practice. We work closely with collaborators across multiple health systems to ensure that the methods we develop are interpretable, actionable, and feasible for deployment in routine care.

We welcome collaborations and inquiries from researchers, clinicians, and students interested in advancing precision medicine through thoughtful and practical computational innovation.

Research

Current Projects

Predictive modeling using electronic health records (EHR)

EHRs represent a large-scale but underutilized resource for early detection. As routinely collected data sources, EHRs capture longitudinal trajectories of diagnoses, medications, procedures, laboratory results, and healthcare utilization. These data can reveal prediagnostic signals and enable personalized risk profiling at scale. Compared to many other emerging early detection technologies such as advanced biosensors, digital imaging, and blood-based liquid biopsy tests, which often face barriers including sample collection requirements, high cost, and limited coverage, EHRs are non-invasive, low-cost, and already embedded in routine clinical care. Leveraging EHRs as a first-line or complementary tool could transform early detection and expand equitable access, particularly in underserved populations.

Multi-cancer prediction using EHR foundation models

Foundation models enable powerful new ways to learn from EHR data. Recent advances in large-scale representation learning have unlocked the potential to derive predictive signals from complex longitudinal health records. Analogous to how large language models such as ChatGPT are trained on vast amounts of text to generate broadly useful representations of language, EHR foundation models are trained on longitudinal patient trajectories to capture transferable clinical representations that can generalize across prediction tasks. Our preliminary work demonstrates that such models substantially improve cancer risk prediction compared with traditional supervised approaches, across multiple cancer types and health systems. These findings establish the feasibility of a unified, scalable, and generalizable EHR-based early detection system.

Interactive system for model explanation

Conversational AI can enhance interpretability and clinician trust. Despite substantial gains in predictive accuracy, clinical adoption of AI models remains limited because outputs are often distorted by noise or bias in underlying data and explained in ways that fail to earn clinician trust. Traditional explainability tools (e.g., SHAP, LIME, DeepLift) frequently surface institution-specific artifacts rather than clinically meaningful factors, while manual feature curation is unsustainable and risks omitting useful signals. To bridge this gap, clinicians need explanations that are both transparent and actionable, reflecting model reasoning in the context of real patient data. Our approach links an EHR foundation model with a conversational interface that generates SOAP-formatted summaries and dialogue-based explanations grounded in the patient timelines used by the model, model-derived features, risk scores, and clinical notes. This allows clinicians to explore why a patient was flagged as high risk, compare predictions across cohorts, and adjust inputs to see how specific interventions might alter a patient's trajectory.
